Beyond Commands: The Art, Science, and Paradox of Prompt Engineering

Read on Medium

Why It Matters More Than You Think

One of the key reasons that led me to write this article was the emerging popularity of prompt engineering roles and the frequent mention of prompt engineering in open job positions. I thought, “We see this everywhere, but do we truly understand what it means?” I realized that I had more than enough experience to share my perspective on prompt engineering, how to develop universal prompts, and how to enhance workflow efficiency. *It’s important to note that this article will not yet discuss Sora, OpenAI’s video generation model. Prompt engineering for Sora is a unique topic that deserves its own dedicated article. Here, we will focus on prompt engineering for text-to-image and text-based AI models.

In the age of AI, where text-to-image models and generative AI tools are revolutionizing creativity and productivity, one concept has emerged as crucial: prompt engineering. While many are familiar with the idea of prompting an AI to generate text or images, few understand the depth and importance of how we craft these prompts. It’s not just about asking AI to create something — it’s about knowing how to ask to get the best results.

The Act of Naming and Categorization: How It Shapes AI’s Reality

One of the most overlooked yet powerful aspects of prompt engineering is the act of naming and categorization. Naming gives something its own identity, while labeling and categorizing help AI understand relationships, context, and structure — critical next steps in prompt engineering. To name something is to give it form in language, which in turn makes it real in a conceptual space. This is a fundamental principle in philosophy, storytelling, and even AI interaction.

Throughout history, many myths and religious texts describe the act of naming as an act of creation:

  • In Genesis, Adam names the animals, defining their existence.
  • In Egyptian mythology, knowing a god’s true name gives power over them.
  • In Norse myths, Odin gains knowledge by naming things, showing that understanding and naming are intertwined.

To name something is to give it meaning and existence — both in human cognition and in AI interaction.

AI models, especially text-to-image models like DALL·E, Midjourney, and Stable Diffusion, are trained on vast datasets of past visual data — they don’t “see” the way we do, but they have memory-like associations of images they’ve encountered before.

When we see a generic prompt like “A futuristic city”, AI pulls from millions of existing data points to generate an image that approximates what is most commonly associated with that term. But when we refine it — “Neo-Aurora, a neon-lit metropolis suspended above stormy oceans, where sky trains glide between floating skyscrapers” — we are forcing AI to build something specific, immersive, and original.

AI relies on patterns, not understanding. It doesn’t ‘see’ the world as we do; it recalls and assembles data based on statistical probabilities.
  1. Naming reduces ambiguity. The more specific we are, the less AI defaults to generic outputs.
  2. We act as AI’s “eyes.” Since AI doesn’t truly “see,” we must describe as if explaining to a blind person who once had vision, helping it anchor ideas to known concepts.

Are We Creating or Replicating?

This leads us to a fundamental question: Is AI actually creating something new, or is it just repackaging past information? Do we create, or do we replicate? In many ways, creativity is an advanced form of mimicry — whether conscious or subconscious. Everything we generate is informed by what we’ve seen, experienced, or imagined. The best prompt engineers — like great artists, writers, and designers — are those who understand how to recombine existing elements in novel ways to produce something that feels fresh and intentional.

So, it’s safe to say, that everything we create is built on pre-existing knowledge and patterns. AI itself does not create — it predicts based on data. Even human artists are influenced by their environment, experiences, and history. True originality comes from recombining existing ideas in new ways.

The difference between copying and creating is intention — choosing what to mix, how to shape it, and what new meaning it gains. AI itself can only replicate patterns until prompted by a human mind that adds a layer of meaning.

Prompt engineering is a blend of technical knowledge, creativity, and structured thinking. Unlike traditional programming, where explicit logic is written for a machine to follow, prompt engineering is about shaping an ambiguous, natural-language instruction into something that produces predictable, high-quality results. A prompt engineer is not just someone who types words into an AI model. They are strategists, artists, and problem-solvers who bridge the gap between human creativity and machine intelligence.

True creativity is often about recombining known elements in a novel way. A good prompt engineer doesn’t just ask for a “sci-fi city” — they curate, refine, and modify AI’s responses to create something that feels new.

What Defines a Good Prompt Engineer?

One of the most fascinating aspects of prompt engineering is how it serves as a mirror of human cognition. Every time we craft a prompt, we externalize our thought process into words, forcing us to clarify, structure, and refine our ideas. Just as language shapes our perception of reality, prompts shape AI’s perception of what we want it to generate.

In this sense, prompt engineers are not just technical operators — they are architects of AI’s perception. When AI struggles with an abstract prompt, it is often because our own thoughts are ambiguous. Writing an effective prompt requires us to think deeply about what we actually want, how we categorize information, and how different concepts interrelate. They are strategists, artists, and problem-solvers who bridge the gap between human creativity and machine intelligence.

✅ Observational & Pattern Recognition Skills → Seeing what works and why.
✅ Structured Thinking → Breaking down ideas into clear, modular prompts. The ability to break down an image, a sentence, or an idea into component parts and analyze what makes it effective.
✅ Creativity & Precision → Balancing imagination with technical control. Understanding how language affects outputs (e.g., how changing adjectives, verbs, and structure affects AI-generated results).
✅ Iterative Testing → Refining prompts based on AI responses. Spotting trends in AI behavior and fine-tuning inputs based on past experiences.

A novice user types randomly and hopes for the best. A good prompt engineer crafts prompts like a film director setting up a scene — intentional, structured, and vivid.

How AI “Sees” the World: Understanding Its Limitations

To become a better prompt engineer, it’s crucial to understand how AI processes visual and textual data. Let’s first break down how we perceive reality and what we see.

How the Human Brain Analyzes a Scene:

  1. Initial Processing — Light enters the eyes, and the brain detects basic shapes and edges.
  2. Higher Processing — The brain recognizes objects, depth, and relationships.
  3. Contextual Interpretation — The brain assigns meaning and emotion based on experience.

How AI “Processes” an Image Prompt:

  1. Pattern Matching — AI finds statistical correlations between words and images it has seen before.
  2. Style & Composition Approximation — It generates blended versions of similar concepts.
  3. Guessing Unknown Elements — When AI lacks information, it fills gaps randomly.

💡 AI lacks true spatial awareness, physics comprehension, and object permanence — which is why we must guide it carefully.

Why Observation Matters and what it takes to create a good prompt

If an AI consistently misinterprets “a dark alley,” a good prompt engineer won’t just write it differently but will recognize what specifically is going wrong (e.g., is it too dark? Does it lack depth? Does it feel horror-like instead of film noir?). They adjust accordingly.

The best prompts balance concise and descriptive elements. Knowing when to be direct (“a 1960s-style diner”) vs. evocative (“a neon-lit roadside sanctuary, frozen in time from the 1960s”) dramatically changes the output. Creativity in reframing requests to overcome model limitations.

Example: Reframing for Precision

Instead of saying:
➡ “A warrior in a battlefield at sunset” (which might be too generic and biased, because 99% it will generate male warriors)

You may think, what if I wanted a female warrior or a cat! Why did it assume I was after some male warriors…? Well, it’s all about data and forming your prompt, so what strong prompt engineer might write besides specifying what they want to see:
✅ “A lone female warrior stands atop a blood-streaked battlefield, golden sunset glinting off her weathered armor, illuminating the distant ruins of a lost empire.”

Why does this work better?

  • It specifies visual depth (foreground: warrior male/female, midground: battlefield, background: ruins).
  • It enhances atmosphere (golden sunset + lost empire adds emotional tone).
  • It refines the style (cinematic and evocative rather than flat).

Knowing what AI struggles with (e.g., hand structure in images, long-form logical consistency in text). Designing prompts that guide AI towards strengths instead of forcing it into weak areas. Being aware of biases and distortions in AI-generated content and adjusting accordingly.

Example: Working Around AI Limitations

If AI struggles to create hands, a prompt engineer might say:
✅ “A pianist’s fingers gracefully hover above ivory keys, the warm candlelight casting elegant shadows over the polished surface.”

Instead of:
❌ “A pianist playing a song on the piano.”

This tricks the AI into avoiding the difficult hand details while still evoking the action.

  • They use modular prompts that allow for easy tweaking instead of rewriting from scratch.
  • They develop frameworks for generating different types of content (e.g., characters, landscapes, narratives).

The AI Feedback Loop: Are We Shaping AI, or Is It Shaping Us?

At first glance, it seems like we are the ones directing AI — after all, we craft the prompts, and AI follows our lead. But what if AI, without intent or consciousness, is subtly guiding us in return? What if, in presenting us with certain aesthetics, styles, or narratives, it is shaping our expectations and influencing our creative instincts?

Even though AI isn’t sentient, it has the power to mold human creativity in ways we may not immediately recognize. Because it generates content based on probability — drawing from vast datasets of existing works — its outputs reflect what is most statistically common. Over time, this repetition of patterns can reinforce certain artistic norms and shape our perception of what is “good” or “right.” (cough.. Midjourney…)

How AI “Influences” Our Choices

  • Reinforcing Familiar Patterns → AI relies on pre-existing data, so it naturally favors dominant aesthetics, styles, and structures. The more we engage with AI-generated content, the more we may start internalizing these trends as creative defaults.
  • Persuasion Through Aesthetics → AI-generated images and texts are designed to be coherent, polished, and visually striking. Sometimes, what AI presents is so compelling that we accept it — even if it wasn’t exactly what we originally envisioned.
  • The Nudge Effect → AI doesn’t dictate decisions, but it subtly suggests creative directions. By frequently generating similar compositions or prioritizing certain design elements, AI can nudge users toward particular artistic styles.
  • Constraining Creative Range → Since AI operates on learned data rather than spontaneous innovation, it tends to work within existing paradigms rather than breaking free from them. If prompt engineers aren’t actively pushing AI toward novelty, it may reinforce conventions rather than encourage true experimentation.

Who Is Really in Control?

This raises an intriguing question: If AI’s outputs shape our expectations and choices, at what point does human creativity become influenced more by AI than by independent thought?

  • Are we guiding AI, or is AI subtly guiding us?
  • Can we truly create something new, or will AI always anchor us to known patterns?
  • If AI is optimized to generate what is most statistically appealing, does that make it harder for us to explore unconventional, raw, or experimental creativity?

A skilled prompt engineer must recognize that AI is not just a passive tool but an active participant in shaping creative and intellectual outputs. Understanding this dynamic means knowing how to differentiate between AI’s statistical predictions and truly intentional creative choices.

At the heart of prompt engineering lies a paradox: the more we rely on AI for creative work, the more we risk AI shaping the way we think, create, and innovate. Recognizing this subtle influence is key to maintaining originality while leveraging AI’s power as a tool for inspiration and efficiency.

A well-crafted prompt is not just about getting AI to generate something that matches our request — it’s also about controlling how much influence AI has over our expectations. A critical question in prompt engineering is:

  • Does AI truly follow our intent?
  • Or does it generate something so visually compelling that we overlook deviations from our original request?

Prompt Engineering vs. Fine-Tuning: Who’s Really in Control?

A great prompt engineer knows that AI isn’t just a simple instruction follower — it’s a complex system that interprets language through pattern recognition. While we like to think we control the output, the reality is that AI often shapes our expectations as much as we shape its results.

One key distinction in AI optimization is prompt engineering versus fine-tuning:

  • Prompt engineering is the art of crafting inputs that guide an AI model’s response. It’s about understanding how the model interprets language and refining prompts iteratively to get the best output. It’s fast, accessible, and doesn’t require modifying the model itself.
  • Fine-tuning goes deeper — it involves retraining the model with new datasets to alter its behavior. This requires more resources and expertise but allows for greater control over specialized tasks.

A skilled prompt engineer can optimize AI performance without needing fine-tuning, but there are limits. AI operates on probabilities, not true understanding, so no matter how well we prompt, we are still working within the model’s learned constraints.

Does AI Follow, or Does It Persuade?

One fascinating effect of AI is that it sometimes generates outputs that don’t match our exact request — but are so visually compelling that we accept them anyway. This raises an important question:

  • Are we truly in control of the AI, or is it subtly guiding us toward what it thinks we want?
  • Does AI follow instructions, or does it persuade us by presenting something we find visually or conceptually appealing?

For instance, you might ask for a “foggy mountain landscape at sunrise”, but AI gives you a dramatic, almost cinematic version with extra glow, richer hues, and stylized details. It may not be what you envisioned, but it’s so aesthetically pleasing that you accept it as better than your original idea.

This is where expectation management comes in. A good prompt engineer doesn’t just instruct AI — they understand how AI interprets, modifies, and sometimes reshapes human intent. Being aware of AI’s bias toward visually striking results helps engineers refine prompts not just for accuracy, but for balance between creativity and control.

At its core, prompt engineering is both an art and a science, a dialogue between human creativity and machine interpretation. While we craft prompts to guide AI, AI in turn refines our expectations, subtly shaping the boundaries of creativity. This evolving interplay challenges us to rethink authorship, control, and originality in a world where artificial intelligence is no longer just a tool but a collaborator.

The paradox is clear: We prompt AI, refining its outputs, sculpting it into something that aligns with our vision. Yet, the more we use it, the more it sculpts us in return. Yet, this does not mean we surrender control. Instead, it calls for a deeper awareness of how AI functions — not as an omniscient creator, but as an extension of human intent.

So where does that leave us? Are we engineers of the future, bending AI to our will? Or are we simply mirrors, reflecting back a world AI has assembled from fragments of human creation? This is the paradox of prompt engineering: a game of control where we are both the players and the played.

A truly skilled prompt engineer is not just someone who extracts high-quality outputs from AI but someone who understands the invisible shaping forces AI exerts over our creative processes.

As AI continues to evolve, the question is no longer just how we prompt AI, but why — and what that means for the future of human expression. The challenge is not merely mastering AI, but ensuring that in this symbiotic relationship, human creativity remains at the forefront. Because in the end, the true power of prompt engineering is not just in making AI work for us — but in making sure we still recognize what makes our own creativity uniquely human.
