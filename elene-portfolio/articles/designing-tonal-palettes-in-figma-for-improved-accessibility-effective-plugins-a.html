Designing Tonal Palettes In Figma For Improved Accessibility: Effective Plugins & Tools To Use

How many of you have come across a design system where colors are labeled with numbers, clearly meant to define them in a specific way? You might have wondered how these numbers are created. Is there a particular rule behind it, or do designers randomly choose them from the color wheel and assign numbers merely to differentiate them?

As a designer, you might be tempted to use the color picker or similar tools for your UI designs, thinking it gives you more freedom. However, not setting up color styles can lead to a lack of consistency and clarity, resulting in a confusing experience for users. _Trust me, taking the time to define a color palette, even a simple one, is a game-changer_. It not only streamlines your workflow but also saves you from headaches later on. _And when it’s time to hand over your designs to devs, they’ll thank you for having a clear, consistent color scheme to follow._ Remember, a well-defined color palette is key to a cohesive and effective design.

So, in this article, we’re going to learn how to create palette, define some key terms, and what those color tones really mean. I’ll show you how I create and check them in Figma and what tools I use, to make the whole thing way smoother and quicker.

Defining values, hues, tints and shades

For those who still find it a bit hard to find the difference between these terms, let’s explain in a very simple way.

Values:When we talk about the ‘value’ of a color, we’re basically talking about how light or dark it is. _Imagine a slider from white to black — moving along this slider changes the value of the color, without changing the actual color itself._ It’s like adjusting the brightness on your screen; the colors stay the same, but how light or dark they appear changes.

color palette from google material design

Hues:Hue is a fancy word for a basic, pure color — e.g. red, yellow, green, and blue are all hues. It’s the purest form of a color, without any addition of white, black, or gray. Think of hues as the starting point for creating any color you can think of.

Wikipedia color hues

Tints:Now, if you take a hue and start adding white to it, you’re creating a tint. It’s like mixing paint — adding white to red gives you pink, right? That’s your tint. Tints are lighter versions of the original hue, often looking softer and pastel-like.

Shades:On the flip side, if you add black to a hue, you get a shade. It makes the color darker. Like if you add black to blue, you get navy. Shades give a depth and intensity to the original color.

We will use shades later.

When it comes to color in design, the first question is whether someone can easily see the color on the screen. However, this is just the beginning. People perceive colors differently, depending on their eyesight, including conditions like color blindness. This means being careful with color combinations to ensure that UI elements are distinct and don’t blend into each other.

Beyond just the color itself, factors like opacity and font-weight significantly impact how a color appears and can affect readability. For instance, using a low opacity might result in a lighter color that’s hard to read, especially for people with less-than-perfect eyesight. A text with only 30% opacity could be challenging to read from a normal distance for someone who is nearsighted.

Opacity should be used thoughtfully, adhering to recommended guidelines. Similarly, font-weight plays a crucial role, especially on darker backgrounds. In print, thin fonts can be problematic as the ink may spread, making the text feel crowded. This effect can also happen on screens, as our eyes try to compensate and fill in the spaces, leading to a claustrophobic feeling.

It’s also important to distinguish between legibility and readability. In this discussion, we focus on legibility, which is about how easily the text can be seen. Readability, on the other hand, is about how easily the text can be understood. Both are key considerations in accessible design.

A little bit about contrast

Contrast in design, essential for creating emphasis, hierarchy, and visual interest, involves varying elements like color, size, and shape. It’s key for making designs accessible, readable, and aesthetically appealing, particularly important for viewers with visual impairments. The World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) recommends minimum contrast ratios for text and UI elements to ensure content is understandable for a broad audience. Contrast not only enhances legibility but also contributes significantly to a brand’s visual identity.

As a basic guideline, aim to keep a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 (as per WCAG AA standards) for all your essential UI elements like text, buttons, and forms. Adhering to the AA level is usually enough to cater to most users, including those with a vision loss equivalent to 20/40. Although, personally I try to follow AAA and whenever I can, I always try to use high contrast (more than 7:1). This ensures that your design is accessible and clear, even for those with reduced contrast sensitivity.

Designing with Accessibility in Mind

Under the latest material design guidelines, the dynamic color system focuses on luminance (or tone, describes the level of light that a digital color value represents)[[1]](https://codelabs.developers.google.com/color-contrast-accessibility?hl=en#4) over hue. This approach allows for contrast visibility through shades, even without hue and chroma. Choosing a color leads to five key shifted colors, creating a tonal palette of thirteen tones, including white and black. The tonal range, from 0 to 100, represents the light intensity in the color.

This tonal palette system ensures inherent accessibility in color schemes. By prioritizing tonality over hex values or hue, color combinations automatically align with accessibility standards. This is crucial for products using dynamic color, as it guarantees compliance with these standards. In terms of readability, contrasting luminance values offer clearer legibility than similar luminance levels.

To ensure colors work well together in different viewing situations, focusing on luminance levels is crucial. This way, colors can blend effectively without the need for the product team to test every single color combination.

Stark

Stark is an all-in-one accessibility tool that integrates with platforms like Figma to simplify creating inclusive digital products. Its key features include a _Contrast Checker for color accessibility, a Vision Simulator for understanding different visual impairments, tools for enhancing navigation with Focus Order and Landmarks, Alt-Text Annotations for better screen reader and SEO compatibility, Typography Analysis for text clarity, and Touch Targets_ to ensure mobile interfaces are easily usable. Stark makes it quick and easy to identify and fix accessibility issues, making digital products more accessible to a wider audience.

As you can see with this all-in-one tool, you can create your own palette using just a single tone.

If you’re designing with a background, a differently colored call-to-action button, and text, the Stark smartly focuses on the text for accurate color comparisons. Need to adjust or explore more color options? Just use the dropdown in the color selection to access all solid colors in your design, neatly organized without duplicates, thanks to their filtering feature.

A11y

A11y contrast checker

Another Figma plugin I frequently use for contrast checking is A11y. I typically use it to double-check for any issues, while Stark is my go-to for active palette and component creation. A11y is great for a final review of all components within a frame. _However, it’s important to note that everything you want to check must be inside the frame, as the plugin won’t run otherwise_. For instance, to check the contrast of a button that’s already a component, you need to nest it within the frame before running A11y. This can be a bit inconvenient, but for quick fixes, it’s still quite a handy tool.

Material Design Theme & Palette Color Generator

Another free and great tool to generate palettes. The AngularJS Material Design framework has a great theming engine but requires custom themes to be formatted specifically, which can be time-consuming. The Material Design Palette Generator simplifies this by allowing easy creation or import of color sets for custom themes. You can quickly design, name, and export your theme to various formats, including JSON and AngularJS Material Design, with future support for Materialize, CSS, Polymer, and others.
