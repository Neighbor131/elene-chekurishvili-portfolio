Introducing SORA: Creating video from text, OpenAI’s new model

Read on Medium

source

Taking another step toward pushing the boundaries of human imagination, I’m sure you’ve thought at least once, “If only I could be a director, I’d shoot a movie about this,” or “That would have been such a cool scene if only I had a camera,” or “I’d probably have recorded that.” Although no one can turn back time, OpenAI has been fast-forwarding us into the future for a few years now. Starting with DALL-E 2, followed by the successful ChatGPT, then multiple GPT models, and now the newly introduced text-to-video model, Sora, OpenAI continues to innovate and expand the possibilities of AI technology.

What is Sora?

Sora is OpenAI’s groundbreaking technology that converts text into videos, essentially acting like a magic wand that brings your stories or descriptions to life as moving images. It can create visuals for a wide variety of scenarios, ranging from everyday situations to imaginative, fantastical scenes. Although Sora is a pioneering tool, it is still undergoing refinement to more accurately understand and visualize complex concepts. OpenAI is dedicated to enhancing Sora’s capabilities and ensuring its responsible use

Who is it for?

Sora is designed for users who want to create realistic and imaginative videos from text. It is aimed at individuals or organizations that require the ability to generate complex scenes, characters, and motions for various purposes, such as storytelling, entertainment, education, or content creation.

What research was used?

Sora builds on past research in DALL·E and GPT models. Specifically, it uses the recaptioning technique from DALL·E 3, which involves generating highly descriptive captions for visual training data. Sora is a diffusion model that uses a transformer architecture, similar to GPT models, and represents videos and images as collections of smaller units called patches. This approach allows for the generation of entire videos all at once or the extension of generated videos to make them longer.

How it works, simply…

Diffusion Model:Sora starts with a video that looks like static noise and gradually removes the noise to create a clear video. _Think of it like starting with a blurry picture and slowly making it sharper and clearer._

Generating Videos:Sora can create whole videos at once or make existing videos longer. _It can keep track of what’s happening in the video over many frames, so even if something goes out of sight for a bit, it still knows it’s there._

The biggest challenge for this model is likely keeping the subject consistent, even when it exits the frame. Anyone who’s used DALL-E models and tried to create characters that stay the same knows how tough it is when they keep changing and never look the same. So, making a model that’s not just text to video, but also keeps characters consistent, is a whole new level of achievement.

Transformer Architecture:Sora uses a special structure called a transformer, similar to what’s used in GPT models (language AI). This allows it to handle lots of data and make videos with high quality.

Patches and Tokens:Videos and images are broken down into small pieces called patches, similar to how words are broken into tokens in language AI. This helps Sora learn from a wide variety of videos and images.

Building on Past Research:Sora uses techniques from earlier models like DALL·E and GPTs. For example, it uses a method from DALL·E 3 to create detailed descriptions for training, so it can better understand and follow text instructions when making a video.

Creating Videos from Text or Images:Sora can make a video based on written instructions, bring a still image to life, or add to an existing video. It pays attention to small details to make the animation accurate.

OpenAI has given us a sneak peek at its capabilities, and they appear very promising. The research paper is being released today, so stay tuned for more details.
