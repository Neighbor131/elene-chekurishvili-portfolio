What I wish I Knew Before I Thought I’d Be Everywhere, Everything, All at Once

Read on Medium

To millennials — aka modern Ulysses…

Because that’s what I was chasing, wasn’t it?

Not just control.
Not just discipline.
But perfection.

The version of me who could do it all.
Be productive, composed, effortlessly thin, endlessly kind, forever reliable.
The one who could smile through hunger, plan through panic, and never need too much.
The one who would never be the burden.
Just the achiever. The caretaker. The strong one.

But no one told me that chasing everything at once means you’re nowhere at all.
You disappear.
Bit by bit.
Piece by piece.
Until all that’s left is the performance.

What I wish I knew is that being everything will break you.
Because it means being nothing for yourself.

You’re allowed to choose one thing.
You’re allowed to rest.
You’re allowed to not answer right away.
To eat. To say no.
To cry in the middle of the day for no “productive” reason.

You’re allowed to not hold it all.

What I wish I knew is that wholeness doesn’t come from being everywhere.
It comes from being here.
Right now.
In your body.
In your truth.
In your breath.

And that, finally, is enough.

But back then, I didn’t know that.
I thought control was healing.
That if I could manage what I ate, how I moved, how I existed — maybe everything else would follow.
Maybe I’d finally feel safe. Seen. Enough.

I didn’t know I was following a script written by fear.
OCD doesn’t always look like rituals you can see.
Sometimes, it looks like the food you eat every day because it feels “right.”
The panic when plans change.
The comfort in counting — because numbers never lie.

People do. Feelings do. I do.

I thought I was building discipline.
What I built was a cage.
And the hardest part?
It worked — for a while.

It got me through the chaos, the noise, the pressure of being a millennial trying to have it all.
We were told to dream big, do more, stay strong.
We made up rules just to cope:
Track everything.
Never stop working.
Shrink yourself.
Smile.

But these rules — these made-up commandments we created to stay afloat — 
they start to own you.
You’re not living with them anymore.
You’re living under them.

And still, somewhere inside…
there’s this tiny voice whispering:
Don’t let go. This is all you’ve got.

That’s the cruel paradox.
Even when the rules hurt, they feel like home.

But like Ulysses, the journey back isn’t about returning to the same place.
It’s about returning changed. Wiser. Weathered. A little softer.
With stories etched in our skin and deeper truths in our pockets.

We built altars to ambition.
Worshipped metrics and hustle like gods.
And now we’re learning to let go.
To unlearn.
To come home — not to what we once knew,
but to who we were before we forgot.

So yes — modern Ulysses.
With to-do lists instead of swords,
burnout instead of shipwrecks,
and the quiet hope that maybe, just maybe,
we’ll finally arrive somewhere we can rest.

But what no one told me is that even safety can become a story you outgrow.

Like every hero’s tale,
there comes a moment you have to leave the familiar.
Leave the comfort.
Leave the part of yourself that thinks you’ll only be loved if you stay small, stay useful, stay perfect.

You have to walk into the forest of your own discomfort.
Your own hunger.
Your own truth.

And ask: What happens if I stop performing?

It’s terrifying.

But then — slowly — you begin to return.

Not to who you were.
But to who you’ve always been, underneath the noise.

You begin to build a new home.
One that doesn’t require punishment to earn peace.
One that allows softness, rest, desire, mess.
One where you don’t have to split yourself into compartments just to be accepted.

And you realize:
You were never meant to be a supporting character in your own story.

You are the hero.
The one who left.
And came back.
Not perfect.
But whole.
