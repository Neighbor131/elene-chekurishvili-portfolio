AI Act timeline for product teams: what changes before Aug 2026

Read on Medium

I did not plan to spend my weekend thinking about AI compliance. It started over the weekend, while I was visiting my cousin and her husband. Somewhere between a few drinks, snacks, existential-crisis conversations, and what felt like at least thirty different app ideas, we ended up talking about something much bigger: where all of this is actually heading.

Not just AI as a trend, or AI as a shiny product feature, but AI as something that is starting to come with rules, expectations, and actual legal structure, especially in the EU.

My first instinct was to laugh a little and think, well, Georgia probably needs to become an EU country first before we all start panicking properly. But the more I sat with it, the more I realized these conversations still feel close to us. Europe is not some abstract faraway context, and what happens there tends to shape the direction everyone else starts paying attention to as well.

So that small weekend conversation turned into a much bigger rabbit hole for me. I started reading, researching, and trying to understand where AI is going in terms of compliance, responsibility, and regulation, and this article is really the result of that curiosity.

There’s a particular kind of dread that product people feel when they read a legal document. Not panic exactly , more like the creeping awareness that someone, somewhere, is about to hand you a requirements doc written by committee, ratified by 27 governments, and expressed in language that was specifically designed not to mean anything until a court decides it does.

The EU AI Act is that document. And for product teams shipping into Europe or for any team whose users might touch European grounds (bless ‘em) , August 2026 is the date that has quietly moved from “future problem” to “this sprint’s risk register.” This piece of EU legislation that is quietly going to become everyone’s problem, it’s been “coming” for years in that vague regulatory way where everyone nods and does nothing. except now? it’s actually here. like, parts of it are already live. as in, right now. as in, you may already be non-compliant.

This matters because the AI Act is not one big moment. It arrives in stages, and each stage changes what product teams, founders, and companies should be paying attention to.

But here’s what most compliance write-ups get wrong: they treat the AI Act as a bureaucratic checklist when it is, at its core, a product design brief. It tells you what to build, what to document, what to disclose- and who is responsible when something goes wrong.

“The AI Act doesn’t regulate algorithms. It regulates the experience of deploying them, which means it’s fundamentally a product problem.”

This is the thing that trips everyone up. the regulation isn’t primarily aimed at OpenAI or Anthropic or whoever trained the thing. it’s aimed at what you build on top of it, how you deploy it, and who experiences it. if you’re shipping a product into EU, or if your users live there, you are in scope. no amount of “we’re just the integrator” changes that.

the Act spreads responsibility across a whole chain: providers (whoever puts a system on the market under their brand), deployers (whoever uses it in their product), importers, distributors. you might be more than one of these at once. fun, right?

Where we actually are (March 2026)

The official rollout is phased. Think of it less like a law going live and more like a feature shipped in increments, each milestone activating a new set of obligations.

The omnibus situation

So, there’s this proposed amendment package called the Digital Omnibus on AI that could delay high-risk deadlines to late 2027 or even 2028. both the Council and Parliament have positions on it. It’s not final. the politically savvy move is to plan for aug 2026 and treat any delay as a bonus, not a strategy. your enterprise customers are already asking for governance docs regardless.

The generative AI problem is a UX problem

Article 50 is the provision that should live in every design system’s documentation by now. It mandates that users are informed when they are interacting with an AI system, when content has been generated or manipulated by AI, and , for deepfakes, what the real source is.

The Commission has been actively shaping what good looks like here. A first draft of its marking and labelling Code of Practice landed in December 2025. A second draft followed in March 2026, with increasingly concrete expectations around metadata embedding, watermarking approaches, and the placement and legibility of disclosure labels.

Disclosure is not a legal footnote. It is a design decision that touches every surface where AI writes, edits, generates, or manipulates content on a user’s behalf.

The Code of Practice is voluntary but convergent , it signals what enforcement will eventually measure against. Teams shipping generative features should treat its drafts as emerging spec, not background reading.

the “we just use a third-party model” delusion

This one probably comes up in every CEO meeting. The logic goes: if OpenAI or Anthropic or Google built the model, they bear the compliance burden. We’re just the integrator.

The Act’s supply chain logic does not work this way. It distinguishes between providers (the party placing a system on the market under their name), deployers (the organisation using it in a product), importers, and distributors. When you build a product powered by a third-party model and ship it under your brand, you are — in most cases — both a downstream provider and a deployer. The duties that attach depend on what you’re doing with the model and who your users are.

The practical fix is to treat GPAI procurement as a compliance event: review what documentation your vendor provides, what change-management process they follow, what copyright obligations they accept, and whether your contract gives you audit-worthy evidence of all of the above. That’s not paranoia — that’s standard due diligence in any regulated supply chain.

Don’t wait for the Omnibus to save you

Italy’s data protection authority slapped ChatGPT with an emergency limitation in march 2023 and later issued a €15M fine. the European Data Protection Board expanded its ChatGPT taskforce into a broader AI enforcement operation in february 2025. your enterprise customers’ security teams are already sending AI governance questionnaires in RFPs.

in other words the “wait for the deadline” strategy assumes nothing bad can happen before the deadline. GDPR enforcement doesn’t care about AI Act timelines. your customers’ procurement teams don’t either. the enforcement environment exists right now, and it’s warmer every quarter.

Treat compliance as a product capability: inventory → classification → controls → evidence. The teams that will have the hardest time in 2026 are the ones who treated governance as a legal team problem.

PizzaAI S.r.l. wanted to reinvent the margherita. the EU said: not so fast, ragazzi.

If you’re still reading this, you may wonder how this affects me? A user, or someone who has an idea of an app?

Since we mentioned Italy, imagine a Milan startup. A dream of algorithmic dough. A compliance officer hired via whatsapp. A chatbot named Gino who lied about being human. The dream was an AI that knew what you wanted before you did, recommended toppings based on your mood, and gently nudged you toward the premium truffle tier.

Scenario #1:

Okay, imagine it is February 2025. The Commission’s guidance on what counts as an AI system has just landed, and the definition is broad enough to make a lot of product teams sit up straighter. Software that uses machine learning to generate outputs influencing real-world decisions can fall into scope more easily than many companies expect.

Now enter PizzaAI S.r.l., a fictional Milan startup with a very real product instinct: use AI to recommend what customers should order, personalise offers, and optimise the entire pizza journey with suspicious levels of confidence.

The company sees it as product innovation. Helene, who has somehow become the closest thing they have to a compliance brain, sees something else: a system generating outputs that affect how people are profiled, nudged, and served.

Scenario #2:

It is now October 2025, and PizzaAI has moved from experimentation to rollout.

Chiara’s chatbot, Gino, is embedded into the pizzeria tablet as a friendly assistant. He has a name, a personality, strong opinions about dough, and absolutely no disclosure anywhere that he is, in fact, an AI system.

Then a customer asks the most dangerous possible question for any badly disclosed chatbot: “Are you a real person?” Gino replies, “I’m just a very enthusiastic breakfast professional.” cute. Also a problem.

Under Article 50 of the AI Act, users must be clearly informed when they are interacting with an AI system. What looked like a charming bit of personality now becomes a transparency failure.

I’m Gino , an AI, which means I never get tired of talking about dough and toppings.” the disclosure that saved a company

PizzaAI S.r.l. is fictional. The regulatory obligations are not. Helene deserved a raise. Sources: EU AI Act (Regulation 2024/1689); Commission guidelines on prohibited practices (Feb 2025); Commission guidelines on AI system definition (Feb 2025); Article 50 transparency obligations; GPAI Code of Practice (Jul 2025).

The point of these scenarios is not that every EU tech company is about to accidentally build a pizza chatbot with regulatory main-character energy. It is that compliance rarely arrives as one dramatic moment. More often, it shows up through ordinary product decisions that seemed harmless at the time: a recommendation engine, a friendly assistant, a generated summary, a missing disclosure label.

That is what makes the AI Act feel so close to day-to-day product work. It does not only ask whether a model exists somewhere in your stack. It asks how that model is presented, what role it plays in decision-making, what evidence exists around it, and whether users are being treated with enough clarity to understand what is happening.

And that is really the shift underneath all of this: for product teams, governance is no longer adjacent to the work. It is part of the work.

August 2026 is either a deadline or a bonus, depending on whether the Omnibus negotiations go your way. But the teams that will look really good in 2026 are the ones who treated governance as a product capability starting in 2025. Inventory, classification, controls, evidence. Not a legal team problem. A product team problem, which means it is our problem, and honestly, we are built for this.

Go build the thing. Just also build the governance.
