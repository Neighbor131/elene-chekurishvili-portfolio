One year of progress: DALL-E 2 Experimental model

Read on Medium

9 min read

Mar 7, 2023

d2 exp

When I received an early invite to OpenAI’s new Dalle model almost 10 months ago, I knew I had stumbled upon something very special. What can I say, it’s been a fascinating journey so far. I’ve spent much of my free time providing feedback and contributing to its development, and I’m impressed and proud of how far it’s come. Dalle2, in particular, was a significant improvement over its predecessor, of course, there have been some issues, but overall d2 did its job pretty well.

Recently, ChatGPT has been getting a lot of attention, and while it’s exciting to see new models emerge, I wished OpenAI didn’t forget about D2. Now, imagine my excitement when I got invited as an alpha tester again.

Since the model is experimental and still in development, I’m not going to write a heavy review, for now, I will stick with listing notable improvements; how prompting works with the new model, image comparisons, and re-generating old prompts.

Improvements

1. Textures(painting mediums), skin, shapes, faces, hair 2. Less cropped images 3. Improved performance when generating hybrids (Centaur, Minotaur, Elves, Cerberus, chimera) 4. Sharp photos 5. humanoid characters 6. Incorporating more details from the prompt

Remaining issues

1. Color allocation, “yo-yo” issue 2. gender bias when creating facial expressions 3. Safety filters false positives

Prompting and the importance of word order

When generating prompts, word order is important to keep in mind. While image-generating models can understand natural language, how you phrase your request still matters. Word order becomes especially significant for more complex prompts, which was an issue with the previous model. The more complex your prompt is, the more confusing it gets for the model to prioritize tasks, usually leading to wrong generations.

To examine the role of word order in prompting, I use the following approach: Using a complex prompt, we compare how changing the wording affects outcomes.

Prompt: A propaganda poster depicting a cat dressed as french emperor napoleon holding a piece of cheese

Let’s break down the prompt element by element:

  • A propaganda poster (determining the style of generation)
  • A cat dressed as French Emperor Napoleon (subject)
  • A piece of cheese object

The prompt specifies generating a propaganda poster featuring a cat dressed as Napoleon.

The prompt is from the Dalle2 research paper

Running the prompt in a new model produced varied, interesting results. The model assumes a piece of the object and generates logically relevant data based on this assumption, rather than considering the object we’ve originally given. As a result, only 1/4 “understood” and included in the image. Moving the object to the starting position affects the final output. Now, half of the images (2/4) depict the cheese correctly.

Rather than starting the prompt by describing the image, we start by describing the subject. As you can see pushing it in the back makes the style lose some attributions, e.g. no more flags. While the model tries to depict the cheese somewhere in the generation, it still struggles to turn the object into an animated subject.

The second picture is what we were originally looking for, to achieve that we had to use additional mediums, and adjectives (humanoid/human/human sized/animated/etc)

Appreciating Ghibli aesthetics

D2 Exp.

After testing a few movies, it seems that the new model performs better in mimicking Ghibli’s iconic color palette, with Kiki’s Delivery Service and My Neighbor Totoro being the best performers.

While I have noticed numerous improvements in the model, it wouldn’t be right to make any final conclusions as it is still in development. Although some minor issues still need to be addressed, they are nonetheless important. One such issue is the “yo-yo” problem, where certain subjects are poorly represented by the AI.

D2 Exp.

Another important issue is color allocation. Essentially, it occurs when the AI becomes confused by the presence of multiple subjects in the prompt, and this confusion only increases when additional subjects are added.

D2 Exp.

Facial expressions and possible bias

The old model exhibited a subtle gender bias in its generation of human expressions. Specifically, when the model leaned towards generating aggressive expressions, it tended to depict male faces, whereas when it produced more subdued expressions, it tended to generate female faces.

Pissaro managed to escape but not Dicksee

A few months ago, I wrote an _article_ on assumptions, biases, and filters. Although the new model is capable of recognizing Pissaro as an artist and not something related to G-rated bodily fluids, it still struggles with certain names and homonyms. Unfortunately, Dicksee couldn’t escape these filters this time either.
