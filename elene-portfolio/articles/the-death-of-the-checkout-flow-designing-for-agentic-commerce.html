The Death of the Checkout Flow: Designing for Agentic Commerce

Read on Medium

You tell ChatGPT to find the best food brand for your neutered cat. It asks a few clarifying questions — age, activity level, diet preferences. Within minutes, it finds a high-protein formula for sterilized cats, checks local stock, applies your loyalty points, and places the order. The next morning, the doorbell rings. Haruka is happy. You’re even happier — because you didn’t do anything except express intent.

That’s where commerce is heading: a world where buying no longer feels like a process.

The checkout flow — once the cornerstone of eCommerce design — is quietly dying, replaced by something more fluid, predictive, and human: Agentic commerce.

Where We Are Now (2025 Snapshot)

Two giants, Walmart and Amazon, are setting the tone.

Walmart recently integrated with ChatGPT to create an Instant Checkout experience. Shoppers can now ask, “Get me a new yoga mat under $30,” and complete the purchase directly in-chat.

A few compelling details:

  • The rollout is “soon,” but no firm date yet.
  • In early trading after the announcement, Walmart’s shares rose ~3%.
  • At launch, Instant Checkout will support single‑item purchases; multi‑item carts are in the pipeline.
  • Walmart frames this as “agentic commerce” — meaning AI will move from reactive to proactive, learning and predicting shoppers’ needs.
  • Walmart is also deepening its internal AI plans: the company is rolling out AI training and certification for employees, including via OpenAI.
Amazon, meanwhile, is testing Buy for Me — an AI that shops on third-party sites — and Lens Live, a visual search that lets you point your camera at any object and instantly find it online.

Both experiments suggest the same future: a world where checkout becomes a conversation, not a process.

Some indicators pointing toward this future:

  • Faster decision cycles: AI reduces friction, so purchases happen more quickly (bypassing drop-offs).
  • Smarter automation: Instead of fixed workflows, AI learns from behavior, adapts, and acts.
  • Trust & risk models: To allow more “hands-off” checkouts, systems will need high trust (authentication, fraud detection, user preferences).
  • Regulation, oversight, transparency: Users will likely want control settings (e.g. how much autonomy to give the AI) and auditability.

Will Manual Checkout Flows “Die”?

Probably not entirely, for multiple reasons:

  1. Edge cases, exceptions & oversight: For unusual orders, controlled goods, large purchases, or legal/regulatory constraints, human confirmation or manual overrides may persist.
  2. Trust & transparency: Many users will still want to see what’s being purchased (especially for expensive items).
  3. Adoption lag & infrastructure constraints: Not every seller, region, or platform will adopt agentic systems quickly — some will stick to known flows.
  4. Security & fraud: More autonomy opens more attack surface; having confirmation steps helps mitigate risk.

Let’s simulate

Stage 1: AI-Assisted Checkout (2025 — 2027)

Today’s chat-based assistants are the bridge between web forms and full autonomy.

Imagine messaging a brand:

Order the same cat food I got last month, but in smaller package.

The AI finds the product, applies coupons if any, confirms shipping, and shows you a single “Approve” button.

Design shift:

UX designers move from mapping linear checkout flows to crafting decision checkpoints — tiny, trust-centric moments inside a fluid conversation.

The challenge is no longer about minimizing clicks, but about designing confidence in one click.

Stage 2: Agentic Commerce (2028 — 2031)

Fast-forward a few years. You’ve given your AI permission to buy within clear rules:

If my running shoes drop below €90, buy them in black, size 38

No checkout appears. The AI executes, and you get a daily digest of its actions.

Design shift:

We stop designing transactions and start designing trust dashboards.

Interfaces show:

  • what the AI did,
  • why it acted,
  • how to undo or refine those decisions.
The new UX question becomes: How do we visualize autonomy without losing control?

What This Means for Designers

  1. From flow to framework: Designers evolve from building steps to setting boundaries.
  2. From clicks to consent: Confirmation screens turn into transparency logs and reversible actions.
  3. From UX to AX (Agent Experience): We’ll need new heuristics for how AIs interpret, explain, and act within design systems.
  4. From frictionless to trustworthy: The future of conversion is not speed — it’s safety.

Manual checkout won’t die completely; it’ll remain for high-value or edge-case purchases. But the default expectation is shifting toward invisible, intelligent commerce.

The Human in the Loop

As with autopilot in aviation, automation doesn’t eliminate humans — it redefines their role.

We’ll still step in for emotional or ethical nuance: surprise gifts, luxury buys, conscious consumption.

The question is not “Can AI handle checkout?” but “When should it?”

Designing for the Invisible

In a few years, the “Buy” button might vanish, but our design responsibility won’t.

We’ll be crafting experiences that balance convenience with consciousness — making automation feel not just effortless, but earned.

Because the future of shopping isn’t about clicking faster.

It’s about trusting the system that buys for you.
