Creative Labour in the Modern Age

Read on Medium

Invisible Brushes: Rethinking Creative Labor in the Age of AI

“An insult to life itself.” This infamous quote from Hayao Miyazaki, spoken in response to a grotesque AI-generated animation, often resurfaces in debates about AI and art. (Highly out of context if I may say, He saw this particular animation he’s referring to not as creative, but as nihilistic — done because they could, not because it expressed anything meaningful or human.) While it reflects Miyazaki’s deep reverence for the soul of animation, it also reveals a broader cultural belief: that creative labor must be visibly difficult to be meaningful. If you’re not suffering for your art, are you even creating?

This thinking is deeply ingrained in our perception of creative worth. The image of the struggling painter, the tortured writer, the designer bent over their desk at 3 a.m. has become romanticized. There’s a certain reverence for the mess — the paint-stained hands, the endless drafts, the visible process of toil. And in contrast, work that looks easy, especially work produced or assisted by digital tools, is often dismissed as less valid.

But what happens when the struggle shifts from the physical to the cognitive? What if the creative labor isn’t in the brushstroke, but in the thinking, the directing, the precise use of language to sculpt an idea into existence?

Imagine this: I generate a hyper-detailed image that looks like a classical oil painting. I didn’t spend 24 hours painting it, but I did spend time imagining the scene, describing it in the right words, refining the prompt, iterating on outputs, adjusting the composition in my mind. I didn’t use a brush — I used my vocabulary, visual literacy, and taste. Is that not labor? Is it not creation?

We are in a transition period. Tools like Chatgpt, Figma’s smart features, and text-to-image platforms are shifting what creative work looks like. And while the tools have changed, the intention behind them hasn’t. The myth we’re battling is that tools which make things easier inherently cheapen the result.

But consider this: a great photographer doesn’t become less great because they use a better camera. A film director isn’t dismissed because they didn’t animate every frame.

These roles are respected for their vision, their ability to guide and shape an idea. Why is it any different when using AI?

There is a bias at play: the mistrust of effortlessness. We associate effort with authenticity, with value. If it looks easy, it must have required no skill. But ease doesn’t mean absence of thought. In fact, creating something meaningful with AI demands new skills — prompting, conceptual thinking, narrative shaping, iteration, curation.

The soul is still human. AI can mimic, remix, regenerate — but it doesn’t imagine. It doesn’t feel. When we use it, we lend it our intention, our taste, our vision. We are still the ones creating.

So maybe it’s time to stop measuring creative labor by how much it hurts and start honoring how much it matters. Maybe the brush is invisible now, but the artist’s hand is still steady, still thinking, still feeling.

Creative work is still work — even if the tools are changing. And as we move forward, we must learn to see the labor within the invisible brushstrokes of a modern creator.

Let’s dive deeper, shall we?

The new image generator from OpenAI

A new image-generation model called o4 has burst onto the scene, quickly becoming a hot topic among artists and designers. Integrated directly into ChatGPT, o4 (also referred to as GPT-4o) is OpenAI’s latest and “most advanced image generator yet”​ (adweek.com) Within days of release, it was so popular that OpenAI’s servers were overloaded​ (adweek.com) . Users have been eagerly pushing o4’s limits — from crafting original art and photorealistic scenes to mimicking well-known animation styles. In fact, generating images in the look of South Park, The Simpsons, Rick and Morty, or Studio Ghibli became a viral social media trend soon after launch (adweek.com). But beyond the buzz and memes, o4 is sparking deeper conversations in the creative community about how AI tools are reshaping creative work. Artists, designers, and thought leaders are asking: What makes this new tool distinct, and how does it enable or redefine creative labor? And more broadly, as AI art tools proliferate, are we witnessing a shift in what counts as “real” artistic labor? This article tries to explore these questions — comparing o4 with other leading AI art generators, and examining how the very notion of creative effort is evolving in the age of AI. Throughout, I will challenge the idea that only traditionally labor-intensive art is valid, highlighting the legitimacy of new forms of creative expression that involve different, often invisible, types of work.

4o’s Impact on Creative Work and Workflows

From day one, many creatives have been impressed by how powerful and accessible 4o is. “It gives a level of creative quality and control to the masses. Previously, this was only available to power users with more convoluted set-ups and workflows,” said Wesley ter Haar, a chief creative officer at a major ad agency​ (adweek.com). In other words, tasks that once required specialized know-how or complex software can now be done by anyone through a simple chat prompt. This democratization of image-making is letting more people participate in visual creation, echoing how digital cameras or smartphones once put photography in everyone’s hands. According to one analysis, “AI-based image generation tools are already accessible to a wide audience, allowing anyone to create visually sophisticated works without traditional artistic training”​ (medium.com). Indeed, 4o’s integration into ChatGPT means you don’t need to install fancy graphics software or learn coding; if you can describe what you imagine, you can start bringing it to life.

Equally important is speed and iteration. Creative professionals note that 4o dramatically shortens the cycle from idea to visual. “The distance between an idea and a fully formed visual is now seconds, not days, and that changes the whole psychology of creativity. You can now think with your eyes,” observed Craig Elimeliah, a creative director at an agency​ (adweek.com). With 4o, a concept that might have taken days of sketching or searching for reference images can materialize almost instantly. This allows artists and designers to brainstorm visually, trying out ideas in real-time. Elimeliah describes a shift “from creativity as craft to creativity as choreography”​ (adweek.com). In their view, using AI like 4o is less about manually executing each stroke of an image (the traditional “craft” labor) and more about directing and curating the outcome — “You’re not just designing an image, you’re directing a system, shaping outcomes with words, taste, and intent”​ (adweek.com). This sentiment is widely shared among early adopters: the skill lies in how you guide the AI, the choices you make, and your artistic judgment, even if the physical effort (typing a prompt) looks minimal. “Those people who will win here aren’t necessarily the best technicians; they’re the ones who know how to guide the machine toward something meaningful,” Elimeliah said​ (adweek.com), underlining that conceptual vision and taste have become the critical “labor” in an AI-assisted workflow.

Not everyone greets 4o with unreserved enthusiasm, however. Some artists worry about over-reliance on AI or the potential devaluation of human skill. On the optimistic side, creative directors like Andrés Ordóñez acknowledge 4o as “a big step forward” that integrates seamlessly into the creative process​ (adweek.com). At the same time, Ordóñez and others caution that it’s not a magic wand to replace all tools or human touch. “It’s not a killer of anything,” notes James Dow, a generative AI director, who sees 4o as “another fantastic tool in the box” rather than a total workflow replacement​ (adweek.com). The more skeptical voices go further — expressing fear that easy AI image generation will undercut professional artists. “I fear this update will embolden the very worst of [artists’] clients… cheapskate companies may feel like they can continue to devalue creative skillsets,” wrote one commentator, worrying that we could be buried in an “AI-slop landslide” of low-effort content​ (pcgamer.com). Similarly, when 4o’s Ghibli-style outputs went viral, some critics derided them as mere “AI slop,” suggesting that their ease of creation and lack of the human touch devalue the painstaking effort behind traditional, hand-drawn animation​ (opentools.ai). These divergent reactions underscore a central tension: o4 undeniably empowers creativity at an unprecedented speed and scale, but it also forces us to grapple with how much we value the process behind the art, not just the result.

What Makes 4o So Powerful (and Different from Earlier Tools)?

4o isn’t just another AI image model — it brings several breakthroughs that set it apart from both its predecessors (like DALL·E 3, 2, 1) and its competitors. OpenAI built 4o as a natively multimodal model, meaning it was trained to handle text and images together in one system​ (medium.com). Unlike earlier generators that simply paired an image model with a language prompt, 4o’s training taught it to understand concepts holistically. The result, as observers note, is that “images are more detailed, lifelike, and aligned with user prompts”​ (medium.com). In practical terms, creators find that o4 follows instructions with remarkable fidelity. Even complex, multi-part prompts — e.g. “create an infographic in the style of X, with a caption that says Y, and incorporate element Z” — are executed with a level of precision that previous models often missed. “GPT-4o image generation excels at accurately rendering text, precisely following prompts, and leveraging [the model]’s inherent knowledge base and chat context,” OpenAI wrote in its announcement​ (openai.com). This is a big deal: earlier AI art tools notoriously struggled with mixing text and imagery (often producing gibberish text in images), and they could easily go off-script from a prompt’s intent. By contrast, 4o can compose an image exactly as described, even including things like legible signage, labels, or UI elements within the scene — tasks that used to trip up the likes of DALL·E or Midjourney​ (pcgamer.com). Another distinctive strength is o4’s ability to maintain context across a conversation. Because it lives inside ChatGPT, you can generate an image, then tell the AI to tweak it or generate variations, all in a continuous thread. “Users can now generate images directly in ChatGPT, refining them through conversation and adjusting details on the fly,” one tech report noted​ (venturebeat.com). OpenAI demonstrated this with examples like designing a living room: the user could request a scene, then ask for changes in lighting, perspective, or art style, step by step​(medium.com)

The model remembers the previous image and instructions, allowing for iterative refinement that feels like collaborating with a human art director. This is fundamentally different from the one-and-done prompting of many other tools. As an artist, this means you can start with a rough idea and then sculpt it through dialogue — e.g. “Now make it nighttime” or “Add a red rug in the foreground” — without starting over from scratch each time. The cognitive load is lower because you’re effectively teaching or guiding the image in stages, closer to how one might art-direct a human illustrator. Creators say this conversational workflow makes the process “intuitive, as users can simply describe what they want, and 4o iterates in real-time” (medium.com). The immediacy of feedback and the ease of making adjustments keep the creative flow going, as opposed to earlier generators where each new image was a separate guess-and-check.

Crucially, 4o also made strides in image consistency and fidelity that professionals had been craving. Early users report it’s better at keeping a coherent style or character across multiple images (useful for storyboarding or comics), and it handles tricky details like photorealism more reliably​ (pcgamer.com)

Competing models often require manual seed control or fine-tuning to get such consistency. Moreover, o4’s integration of a “knowledge base” means it can draw on factual information or world knowledge in creating images​(openai.com). It understands references and can incorporate them correctly (within content policy limits) — whether that’s designing a historically accurate costume or laying out a scientific diagram. In summary, what makes o4 powerful is precision plus ease: it marries the imaginative leaps we expect from AI art with a new level of obedience to the creator’s intent. As one journalist put it plainly, o4 “refines a number of things AI image generators have historically struggled with, such as photorealism and rendering legible text”​ (pcgamer.com). The upshot is that artists spend less time wrestling against the tool’s quirks and more time realizing their vision.

Comparing 4o with Midjourney, Dall-E (previous versions), Stable Diffusion, and Adobe Firefly

How does 4o stack up against other leading AI art tools, and what kind of effort does each require to get top-notch results? Each platform has its own “personality” and workflow, which in turn demands different cognitive skills from the artist:

  • Midjourney: Midjourney is renowned for its stunning artistic output — often highly detailed, well-composed, and aesthetically pleasing by default. Many artists love Midjourney for its “trained eye” in creating “stylized, expressive imagery”​ (adweek.com). It’s often the go-to for concept art, fantasy scenes, and polished illustrations. However, Midjourney runs via Discord (and now a web app) and relies on specific prompt syntax and parameters. There is a bit of a learning curve to master its commands, use its blend/upscale tools, and to understand its style biases. “Midjourney still leads the way in terms of visual craft,” noted creative director Andrés Ordóñez, citing that Midjourney’s editor features let you “zoom into a specific part of an image, refine it with precision, and maintain visual consistency” across variations​ (adweek.com) In practice, getting the best from Midjourney may require iterative prompt engineering — using short descriptive phrases, adding artistic tags (like --v5.2 versions or style keywords), and cherry-picking from multiple outputs. The cognitive effort here is often in experimenting: you might try dozens of prompts or adjustments to nail a vision. That said, Midjourney’s strong default aesthetics can mean less effort in some cases (it “knows” how to make things look good), but more effort when you need exact control. As Ordóñez explained, he might use OpenAI’s o4 to first “shape an idea and generate better prompts to use on Midjourney,” then switch to Midjourney “to refine and elevate the craft [and] push the aesthetic”​ (adweek.com)
    This highlights Midjourney’s role as a visual specialist — superb for rich visuals if you’re willing to guide it with precise prompts and possibly do some manual post-editing.
  • OpenAI DALL·E (previous versions): Before 4o, OpenAI’s main image model was DALL·E 2, and later DALL·E 3 (which launched in 2023 and was integrated into ChatGPT). DALL·E 3 made a splash by allowing natural-language prompting via ChatGPT, vastly improving prompt understanding. It gained praise for faithfully following complex descriptions, but some artists felt its rendering quality still lagged behind Midjourney’s in terms of pure artistry. As one Reddit user bluntly put it, “DALL-E 3 images are amateurish compared to Midjourney’s … The only thing DALL-E 3 is better at is text”​ (reddit.com)
    Indeed, DALL·E 3 introduced the ability to generate readable text in images and showed better adherence to narrative prompts, similar to 4o’s strengths. However, DALL·E 3 was a separate system and often produced somewhat flat or quirky results unless heavily steered. Users reported various “pitfalls in the quality of its images”​ (adweek.com), ranging from distorted hands to odd compositions, requiring patience to correct. By contrast, 4o has been praised as more sophisticated, yielding “more realistic and accurate imagery”​ (adweek.com)
    Another advantage of o4 is the unified chat integration — whereas DALL·E 3 in ChatGPT could take instructions, o4 goes further with continuous refinement and image uploads for reference. In short, compared to its predecessors, o4 reduces the effort needed to get a correct, high-fidelity image. The user no longer has to fight as much against errors or repeat themselves; the AI largely “gets it right” on the first few tries, and if not, you can clarify in natural language. This makes the conceptual effort feel more like a dialogue (as mentioned) and less like clever prompt hacking. OpenAI essentially bridged the gap with Midjourney on image quality while extending ease-of-use — making o4 a strong all-rounder for both casual and professional use. It’s telling that one industry review ranked “DALL-E 3 [now o4] best for casual and professional use through ChatGPT,” whereas “Midjourney [was the] top choice for artistic and stylistic images” requiring more hands-on control​(linkedin.com)
  • Stable Diffusion: Stable Diffusion is the open-source alternative that developers and technically inclined creators champion. Its main strength is customizability. Because SD can be self-hosted and fine-tuned, artists can train it on specific styles or even their own artwork. It’s the backbone of many experimental tools and bespoke workflows. The trade-off is a steeper learning curve and setup. As a LinkedIn comparison notes, Stable Diffusion is “ideal for developers and custom applications” but “image quality depends on fine-tuning and setup”, with a “steeper learning curve” than turnkey systems​ (linkedin.com)
    To use SD effectively, one often must juggle technical concepts like model checkpoints, negative prompts, or CLIP guidance, and possibly use image editing techniques (inpainting, ControlNet, etc.) to get complex results. In terms of cognitive effort, Stable Diffusion demands a more analytic and technical approach — you benefit from understanding how the model works under the hood. For an artist, this can mean investing significant time into prompt experimentation, adjusting dozens of settings, or writing Python scripts if automating a batch of images. However, the reward is flexibility: you’re not constrained by a provider’s rules (aside from your hardware limits), and you can integrate AI image generation into pipelines (for game development, design apps, etc.). Compared to 4o, which aims to streamline the process, Stable Diffusion can feel “power-user”-centric. It gives you the freedom to be very granular (even painting over noise itself) at the cost of convenience. Many in the community use SD for its creative control and as a playground for innovation, while using tools like o4 or Midjourney for quick ideation. In summary, Stable Diffusion shifts more cognitive load onto the user to fine-tune and polish outputs — the labor here is in mastering the tool itself, not just conceiving the art.
  • Adobe Firefly: Firefly is Adobe’s entry into generative AI art, and it’s designed with creative professionals in mind. Unlike the standalone AI models, Firefly is deeply integrated into Adobe’s Creative Cloud suite (Photoshop, Illustrator, etc.). One of Firefly’s distinguishing features is its focus on design tasks and reliability: it was trained on Adobe Stock and other licensed images to ensure generated content is safe for commercial use (no copyright landmines)​ (linkedin.com).
    Firefly also excels at things like text effects and branding imagery — for example, turning a word into stylized art or generating background graphics for layouts. The cognitive effort with Firefly is somewhat different because it’s often used within an existing creative workflow. A graphic designer might use Firefly to fill a background or generate variations of an illustration directly in Photoshop via the Generative Fill tool. This means the artist is still actively composing and editing in real time, using the AI as a helper. Firefly’s interface is user-friendly with sliders for styles and aspect ratios, making it feel like an extension of familiar tools. As a result, Firefly requires less “prompt hacking” — it’s optimized for straightforward instructions, and many options are point-and-click. However, it may be less flexible for outlandishly creative scenes than something like Midjourney, since it’s “focused on design and branding” use cases​ (linkedin.com)
    In terms of conceptual effort, Firefly often augments traditional creative labor rather than replacing it. The designer still decides the overall layout and may manually tweak the AI output. You could say Firefly keeps much of the visible labor intact (one still works in Photoshop layers), but speeds up or simplifies portions of it (like generating a quick concept background, which you then refine). For artists who already have a clear vision, Firefly can save time on execution; for those exploring ideas, it’s a handy aid but perhaps not as generative or “surprising” as 4o or Midjourney can be. One key difference is trust and consistency — professionals might choose Firefly when they need legally usable, dependable results integrated with other assets. The effort here lies in combining AI output with one’s design expertise smoothly.

In summary, 4o vs. the rest comes down to versatility and ease versus specialized excellence. o4’s big advantage is that it combines many strengths in one: it’s conversational and easy for beginners, yet capable of high-quality, precise results that even pros respect. Midjourney might still produce the most jaw-dropping art with the right prompts, Stable Diffusion offers ultimate control if you’re willing to work for it, and Firefly seamlessly augments professional design pipelines. But 4o is blurring the line between these, offering a single tool where you can brainstorm freely, refine with knowledge and context, and get results that are polished enough for serious use. As Ordóñez noted, the real creative revolution may be in “how we combine [tools] to supercharge the creative process, move faster, think sharper, and visualize ideas with more clarity than ever before” (adweek.com) In practice, artists are already mixing o4 with tools like Midjourney or Photoshop — using each where it’s strongest — which suggests that the future of AI art isn’t about one tool to rule them all, but a creative toolkit that expands what each creator can do.

Redefining ‘Creative Labor’ in the Age of AI

Let’s go back to where we started, perhaps the most profound conversation around tools like 4o is how they are changing our notion of creative labor. Traditionally, creating art — whether a painting, an illustration, or a graphic design — has been associated with visible, tangible effort: hours of sketching, mastering physical techniques, painstaking refinement by hand. In the public imagination, “real art” often equated to hard work in a physical or technical sense (think of a sculptor chiseling marble or an oil painter layering strokes on a canvas). AI generators disrupt that notion by making the execution of an image extremely fast and (seemingly) easy. This raises a challenging question:

If an artwork is created in seconds by an AI at a prompt, is there real human labor or creativity involved?

Many in the creative community are pushing back against the assumption that faster or less hand-on execution means “less creative” or “less valid.” They argue that the labor has shifted, not vanished. Instead of laboring with a brush or pixel by pixel, artists are now investing effort in ideation, composition, and curation of AI outputs. As we saw, using 4o effectively is a skill of its own — it requires crafting a good prompt (which involves imagination and clarity), guiding the AI through iterations, and critically evaluating the results to pick the best or refine further. “You’re not just designing an image, you’re directing a system with words, taste, and intent,” Elimeliah emphasized​ (adweek.com), highlighting that the invisible work (choosing the right words, envisioning the outcome, selecting meaningful details) is very much part of the creative process. In fact, some creators liken prompt-based image generation to directing a photoshoot or conducting an orchestra — the art isn’t in clicking the button, it’s in what happens before and after that click. The final output may not show the dozens of prompts tried, the subtle tweaks, or the artist’s mental map of what they wanted, but all that is real effort. “Creativity as choreography,” not craft, still requires a choreographer​ (adweek.com) Thought leaders note that this shift mirrors past transformations in art. When photography was invented, for example, people argued it wasn’t “real art” because a machine did the work of capturing the image. Yet over time, photography came to be understood as its own art form, with its own creative labor (choosing subjects, framing, lighting, developing, etc.). Similarly, a recent analysis pointed out that “Like photography, AI has the potential to further democratize artistic creation… allowing anyone to create visually sophisticated works without traditional artistic training”​ (medium.com). This democratization is double-edged: it empowers new creators, but it also “blurs the boundaries between professional artists and amateurs”, as the barrier to entry lowers (medium.com). Some worry that if “anyone” can do it, then skilled artists will struggle to differentiate themselves, and the market could be flooded (a “saturation of the digital art market,” as one author put it) (medium.com). These are valid concerns, but they don’t negate the creative value of the work itself. History suggests that when a technology automates one part of art, artists refocus on areas where human creativity is still paramount. For instance, once photography could capture reality, painters explored impressionism and abstract art — realms where the human touch and interpretation mattered more​ (medium.com). In the context of AI, we may see artists put more emphasis on originality of concept, personal storytelling, or multi-modal creations that weave together text, image, and even interactivity — things that off-the-shelf AI alone can’t provide without guidance.

Importantly, many artists and commentators assert the legitimacy of AI-assisted art by highlighting the effort and decision-making that goes into it. “The true game changer isn’t one tool… it’s how we combine them to supercharge the creative process,” Ordóñez said, stressing that human creativity is in orchestrating tools in service of an idea​ (adweek.com). Far from being passive, artists must be more strategic and discerning when so much is possible so quickly. As one Reddit user mused, AI is a tool that “allows artists to better express their visions more rapidly and efficiently, rather than replacing real creativity”​ (reddit.com).

In other words, the vision and concept still come from the human; the AI just accelerates the rendering. There is also a growing recognition of new kinds of labor behind AI art that often go unseen. For example, curating a large batch of AI outputs to find the truly inspired ones can take significant time and a keen eye — similar to a photographer taking 100 shots to get 1 great photo. Additionally, artists often do post-processing on AI images (in Photoshop or by hand painting over) to get the exact result they want, integrating the AI’s contribution with human refinement. All this work might be “invisible” to an outside observer who only sees the end picture, but it is part of the creative labor. “How will the concept of artistic skill evolve in a world where AI can generate complex images on command?” one essay asks​ (medium.com). The answer emerging is that artistic skill is evolving toward conceptual and editorial mastery — knowing what to ask for, recognizing what works, and shaping the narrative or message around the image. These are skills that AI alone does not have; they reside in the artist.

The worry is that if the labor isn’t apparent, the audience might not appreciate the art as much, and artists’ livelihoods could be threatened. There’s also the ethical side: is it fair to train AI on human-made art and then have AI output compete with those humans? These issues are being hotly discussed on platforms from ArtStation to industry forums. Yet even among cautious voices, there is an understanding that AI is here to stay in art, and so the focus is shifting to how to integrate it responsibly. Many artists are now advocating for attribution and compensation systems (so that if AI uses their style, they get credit or payment) — which frames AI art not as a rejection of human labor, but as something built on collective human labor that needs acknowledgement​ (digitalmusicnews.com),(chatgptiseatingtheworld.com)

In essence, the broader shift is toward seeing creativity as a collaboration between human and machine. The machine handles more of the grunt work, but the human steers the ship.

The theme of “invisible labor” is central here. Just because AI art production doesn’t involve sweat and paint stains does not mean it involves no labor. It’s invisible in the sense that it’s mental and digital: researching references, trial and error with prompts, refining ideas based on model limitations, and so on. As we challenge the idea that only visibly difficult art is genuine, we can recall earlier art forms that faced skepticism. Digital art created in Photoshop was once derided by some traditional painters as “cheating,” yet now it’s fully accepted as a valid medium — no one would say a digital illustrator isn’t an artist simply because Ctrl+Z (undo) exists to fix mistakes. The value of art has always been more than the toil; it’s about communication, emotion, innovation. If an AI-assisted piece conveys something powerful or shows genuine creativity, the fact that the artist used new tools should not diminish its worth. Creative labor is simply taking on new shapes. As one marketing expert put it, we’re witnessing “the creative process itself being reshaped by the integration of AI” — and it will be up to us to recognize the artistry in how humans work with these tools (christopherspenn.com)

Conclusion

The emergence of 4o and similar AI image generators is changing the landscape of creative work in real time. These tools are undeniably powerful — o4 can conjure up visuals with precision and speed that feel almost magical — and they are being eagerly embraced by many for the new possibilities they unlock. At the same time, they are forcing creatives and audiences alike to re-evaluate what it means to “create” something. As we’ve seen, o4 has been largely welcomed as a game-changer that makes high-level visual communication more accessible​ (adweek.com), allowing artists to iterate faster and focus on their ideas rather than on technical drudgery. In comparing o4 to Midjourney, DALL·E, Stable Diffusion, and Adobe’s Firefly, we find that each has its niche, but o4 stands out for lowering the barrier to entry while still delivering quality — it invites more people to participate in image-making and invites seasoned creators to rethink their workflows.

Most importantly, the conversation around o4 is illuminating a broader evolution in creative labor. We’re moving toward a paradigm where creative value lies not in how hard you sweat, but in how imaginatively and thoughtfully you can direct computational power toward an artistic goal. The labor may be less visible, but it’s no less real: it resides in the crafting of prompts, the selection of outputs, the storytelling that ties them together, and the personal touches added along the way. In challenging the notion that only traditional, manual artistry is “legitimate,” the creative community is highlighting new kinds of effort — call it mental craftsmanship or digital curatorship — that go into AI-assisted art. And as with every technological leap in art (from the camera to Photoshop), those who blend human creativity with the best tools of their era tend to expand the very definition of art.

There’s an ancient idea shared across religious and philosophical traditions — that creation begins not with the hands, but with the mind, the word, the name. In Genesis, Adam names the creatures. In the Gospel of John, the world begins with the Word. Today, as artists guide generative tools with language, we return — consciously or not — to that lineage: creating not just with pigment or pixels, but with meaning. This isn’t an escape from labor. It’s a return to a deeper form of it

In the end, art has always been about ideas and expression. Whether the paintbrush is physical or digital or an AI model, the artist’s role remains: to envision something meaningful and make the countless choices that bring it to fruition. Tools like o4 are simply the latest brushes we have been handed. The true measure of creative labor is not the blisters on one’s hands, but the impact and originality of the creation. By recognizing the skill and intent behind AI-powered works, we affirm that new forms of art are as valid as traditional ones, and that creative labor — however transformed — still underpins them. As one art critic noted in the context of AI, the history of photography shows why the terms of the debate are wrong — the question isn’t whether it’s art, but what new artforms and opportunities it creates​ (noemamag.com). With 4o and its peers, artists are already seizing those opportunities, proving that innovation, not imitation, is the heart of creative labor, no matter how visible (or invisible) the process may be.
